\begin{abstract}
Did \btc{} shift from medium of exchange to store of value between
2021 and 2026? This paper answers with transaction data rather
than exchange prices or survey evidence. A \texttt{dumptxoutset}
chainstate snapshot at height 950{,}696 contains 165.35 million
unspent transaction outputs (UTXOs) and 20.03 million BTC. In that
snapshot, 60.5\% of current BTC has not moved on chain for at least one
year, 43.0\% for at least three years, and 33.3\% for at least five
years. A one-year Bitcoin Core block walk over 55{,}891 blocks
adds the activity view: recent transaction output sums have a small
retail center but a settlement-sized upper tail. The address-level parse
maps 18.31 million BTC, or 91.4\% of the snapshot balance, to 56.21
million canonical addresses; address balances are highly concentrated
(Gini 0.988), and UTXO frequency reveals a split between ordinary
single-output addresses and service-like consolidation addresses. In the one-year address analysis, 20.7\% of addresses had at least one current UTXO created within the past year, and those addresses held 82.2\% of address-attributable BTC. The
verdict is scoped but direct: the base layer evidence is more consistent
with savings, custody, and settlement infrastructure than with a daily
payment rail.
\end{abstract}

\section{The Question}\label{sec:intro}

This paper asks whether \btc{}'s actual function shifted toward store
of value or medium of exchange between 2021 and 2026
\cite{nakamoto2008bitcoin, yermack2015bitcoin, liu2021risks,
makarov2020trading}. The distinction matters because the base chain can
serve several economic roles at once. It can be a retail payment rail, a
settlement system, a custody and treasury layer, a speculative transfer
network, or a dormant savings ledger. Price data alone cannot identify
which role dominates on chain. The evidence horizon is therefore explicit:
the macro/protocol analysis covers 2021--2026, while the chain
analysis uses live UTXOs, one-year block transactions, and
one-year/current-output address-level balances derived from Bitcoin
Core data.

The sample question spans a period with unusually strong economic and
technical forcing. It includes the 2021 risk peak, the 2022--2023
monetary tightening cycle, the FTX trust shock, US spot exchange traded
fund approval, the fourth halving, Mt.\,Gox creditor distributions, and
the Taproot family of protocol upgrades. These events changed the cost of
holding risky assets, the credibility of intermediated custody, and the
availability of regulated access. If \btc{} became more like savings and
settlement infrastructure, that change should be visible in the stock of
coins that do not move, in the size distribution of transactions that do
move, and in the address-level structure of current balances.

The evidence points toward store-of-value use rather than
daily-payment use. The chainstate evidence shows storage: much of the
spendable BTC has not moved for at least one, three, or five years. The
one-year block evidence shows settlement activity: most transaction
output sums are small, but the largest transfers remain very large. The
address analysis shows the same pattern at the address level, with BTC
balances and UTXO counts concentrated even without wallet or entity
clustering. The claim is therefore scoped but direct: the measured stock,
flow, sink, and one-year address evidence support the store-of-value
interpretation; a longer archival run would extend the flow and address
analysis to the full 2021--2026 window.

The contribution is not a new deanonymization method. Prior work already
showed that address graphs, multi-input heuristics, and clustering can
reveal important structure in cryptocurrency systems
\cite{meiklejohn2013fistful, reid2013analysis, androulaki2013evaluating,
harrigan2016unreasonable, goldfeder2018cookie}. This paper instead uses
minimal assumptions to answer a functional question. It treats the base
chain as accounting data, keeps the unit of observation explicit, and
separates direct chain evidence from owner, wallet, or motive claims that
the data do not identify.

\section{Related Measurement Work and Identification}\label{sec:related}

Bitcoin measurement has two recurring tensions. The first is the tension
between exact chain data and behavioral interpretation. A full node can
report whether an output is unspent, when it was created, which script
family locks it, and which transactions appear in one-year blocks. Those
facts are exact for the chain view. They do not by themselves say
whether a person intended to save, trade, pay, consolidate, or rotate a
vault. Work on address clustering and entity attribution tries to close
part of that gap, but it also adds assumptions that can fail when wallets
change behavior or custodians aggregate users \cite{meiklejohn2013fistful,
moser2017anonymous, kalodner2020blocksci, moser2022resurrecting}.

The second tension is between transaction flow and current stock.
Payments are naturally flow concepts: how often coins move, how large
transfers are, and whether outputs are quickly spent. Store of value is
naturally a stock concept: how much value remains still, how old the
live outputs are, and how concentrated the live balances become. A
transaction dataset can support both views, but only if the denominator
is clear. A transaction sample answers what happens when coins move. A
UTXO snapshot answers what currently remains spendable and unspent. An
address-level aggregation answers how that current stock is distributed
across script-derived address strings, not across economic owners.

This paper follows a partial-identification approach in that spirit
\cite{manski1990nonparametric, manski2003partial}. We do not label
wallets, exchanges, merchants, or lost coins. We ask what conclusions are
forced by Bitcoin Core data even under conservative interpretation.
If one lens alone could be explained away by a blind spot, the verdict
requires the other lenses to point in the same economic direction. A
large old UTXO stock, a one-year window settlement tail, and concentrated
current address balances are not the same statistic. Their agreement is
therefore more informative than any single number.

\section{Macroeconomic and Protocol Setting}\label{sec:setting}

The setting matters because the same chain can look like a payment rail
in one environment and a settlement layer in another. From 2021 to 2026,
\btc{} moved through a rate cycle, a trust shock after FTX, regulated ETF
access, the fourth halving, and a new wave of custody tooling around
Taproot. Figure~\ref{fig:macro-timeline} puts those events on one time
axis so the chain evidence is read against the same historical clock.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig01_macro_protocol_timeline.pdf}
  \caption{Macroeconomic and protocol timeline, 2021--2026.}
  \label{fig:macro-timeline}
\end{figure}

The economic story is a gradual change in who had reason to hold coins on
the base chain. The sample begins near the 2021 retail risk peak, when
\btc{} traded with other speculative assets. Tightening then made that
trade more expensive: from March~2022 to July~2023 the Federal Reserve
raised rates by 525 basis points, and the FTX collapse turned the
exchange sector itself into a trust problem. In 2023, regional bank
stress made self custody and scarce collateral more salient, while the
Ripple and Grayscale decisions made a regulated market structure more
realistic. By January~2024, spot ETF approval changed access from mostly
direct exchange accounts to regulated brokerage and custody channels. The
halving, the start of Mt.\,Gox distributions, the first Fed cut, and the
2025 CLARITY Act process all arrived after that institutional access
point, not before it. The timing matters: the market no longer had to
rely only on retail trading demand to absorb supply \cite{ftxbankruptcy2022,
secripple2023, grayscalesec2023, sec2024etfapproval, mtgoxtrustee2024}.

The technical story is quieter but just as important. Bitcoin did not
change its monetary rule in this period. What changed was the practical
tooling available to large holders. Taproot activated at block~709{,}632
on 2021-11-14, bringing Schnorr signatures, Taproot outputs, and Tapscript
to production \cite{bip340, bip341, bip342}. MuSig2 then made many-party
signing look like an ordinary spend on chain, while Miniscript, PSBT, and
hardware wallet support made those signing policies easier to operate
\cite{bip327, nick2020musig2}. At the same time, Ordinals, BRC-20, and
Runes showed that blockspace was not only a retail payment channel; it
was also being used for issuance, data, and settlement-style activity
\cite{rodarmor2023ordinals}. Conservative consensus combined with more
mature custody tooling is exactly the environment in which base-layer
coins can become slower moving.

Together, these conditions make a store-of-value interpretation plausible
before looking at the measurements. A higher-rate environment removed
much of the easy retail leverage. ETF approval and custody tooling
created channels for large, slower moving balances. Taproot and MuSig2
made institutional signing less visible and easier to coordinate. The
chain evidence below asks whether the actual UTXO set, one-year blocks,
and address-level distributions are consistent with that shift.

\section{Data and Evidence Boundary}\label{sec:data}

The measurements use Bitcoin Core chainstate and block data. The stock
and sink sections use the chainstate snapshot at height 950{,}696. The
flow section uses a one-year block window, covering heights
894{,}806--950{,}696, or 2025-05-02 through 2026-05-23. The address
section uses one-year/current-output address summaries and
current-balance concentration.

Together these data support three claims: the current BTC stock is old,
recent base-layer movement has a settlement-sized upper tail, and the
current address layer is concentrated and operationally heterogeneous.
The macro setting covers 2021--2026; the address-level evidence in this
paper is one-year/current-output analysis.

\section{Measurement}\label{sec:design}

The empirical test uses stock, flow, sink, and address views of the same
base layer. The first three are the primary functional lenses. The fourth
is the address-level decomposition that checks whether the conclusion is
only a side effect of aggregate UTXO age.

A few definitions fix the units before the measurements. A UTXO is an
unspent transaction output: one spendable coin fragment created by a past
transaction. BTC balance is value, measured by summing the BTC amounts in
UTXOs. UTXO count is not value; it is the number of unspent outputs. An
address can therefore have high BTC balance with one large UTXO, or high
UTXO count with many tiny UTXOs. BTC share reports a share of value, while
UTXO-count share reports a share of output pieces. Address recency is
the age of the newest current UTXO assigned to that address.

Let $H$ be the chain tip height and let $\mathcal{U}_H$ be the UTXO set at
that tip. For output $u\in\mathcal{U}_H$, let $b_u$ be its BTC value,
$h_u$ its creation height, $s_u$ its output script family, and $a_u$ its
canonical address string when the parser can assign one. Stock and sink
use the same UTXO-age calculation. The stock lens reports BTC by age
bucket; the sink lens reports cumulative shares past fixed age cutoffs:
\begin{equation}
  S(\tau)=\frac{\sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}_H} b_u\,\mathbf{1}\{H-h_u\ge \tau\}}
                {\sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}_H} b_u},
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a block-age cutoff.

For the one-year flow window $\mathcal{B}$, let $x_t$ be the non-coinbase
BTC output sum of transaction $t$. The flow lens estimates monthly
quantiles
\begin{equation}
  Q_{m,p}=\inf\left\{q: \frac{1}{|T_m|}\sum_{t\in T_m}
  \mathbf{1}\{x_t\le q\}\ge p\right\},
\end{equation}
where $T_m$ is the set of one-year window non-coinbase transactions in
month $m$. These are output-sum quantiles before change-output correction.
They answer how large visible base-layer movements are in the
one-year window.

For address analysis, define the set of canonical addresses
$\mathcal{A}=\{a_u: a_u\neq \emptyset\}$. For address $a$, current balance
and current UTXO count are
\begin{equation}
  B_a=\sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}_H:a_u=a} b_u,\qquad
  C_a=\sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}_H:a_u=a} 1.
\end{equation}
The address balance Gini is computed over the non-negative vector
$\{B_a:a\in\mathcal{A}\}$, and the UTXO-count Gini is computed over
$\{C_a:a\in\mathcal{A}\}$. These two quantities answer different
questions. $B_a$ measures how much BTC value sits at an address. $C_a$
measures how many current output records sit at that address. Address
dormancy uses the most recent current UTXO at an address. This is
intentionally conservative: if a large old address receives one recent
output, the entire address is classified as more recent, so the
old-address balance statistic is a lower-bound style summary of visibly
unused address balances.

\section{Stock Lens: Balance \texorpdfstring{$\times$}{x} Dormancy Partition}\label{sec:stock}

We first measure the stock of coins that look more like savings than
working balances. A UTXO is an unspent transaction output: a coin fragment
that was created by an earlier transaction and has not yet been spent by a
later one. The UTXO set is therefore the live inventory of spendable BTC
at a given chain tip. Bitcoin Core's \texttt{dumptxoutset} RPC serializes
that live inventory to a file and reports the snapshot base height and
hash \cite{bitcoincore2026dumptxoutset}. The snapshot used here is
recorded at height 950{,}696.

The parser read 165.35 million UTXOs from that snapshot, totaling
20.03 million BTC. For each output, the parser kept the BTC amount and
the height $h$ of the transaction that created it. Age is computed by
comparing that creation height with the snapshot height: $(950{,}696-h)/144$
days. We then put each output into an age bucket and sum BTC inside each
bucket. An old UTXO therefore means that the output has not moved on chain
since its creating transaction.

The BTC weighted buckets are large. At the snapshot date, 12.13 million
BTC, or 60.5\% of the UTXO set total, is at least one year old. Coins at
least three years old account for 8.61 million BTC, or 43.0\%; coins at
least five years old account for 6.68 million BTC, or 33.3\%.
Figure~\ref{fig:stock-lens} shows the distribution. That is already hard
to square with a base layer dominated by day-to-day payments: much of the
current stock has simply not moved for years.

The blind spot is motive and ownership. UTXO age is not the same as an
economic holding period. A coin can change hands inside a custodian or
through a legal claim without moving on chain, and a vault rotation can make
a patient holder look young. We therefore read the bucket totals as a stock
aggregate: how much current BTC has stayed still long enough to resemble
savings.

\section{Flow Lens: Transaction Size Distribution}\label{sec:flow}

For flow, we ask what a base chain transaction looks like when coins
move. Unlike stock and sink, this lens studies activity rather than the
full chainstate supply. Its denominator is the one-year block window: all
non-coinbase transactions in the blocks that the one-year block window
provided. Bitcoin Core provided those one-year blocks through \texttt{getblockchaininfo}, \texttt{getnetworkinfo},
\texttt{getblockhash}, and \texttt{getblock}. For each one-year block,
the script drops the coinbase transaction and sums the BTC outputs of
every other transaction. It then groups transactions by month and records
the median, p90, and p99 output sums. Figure~\ref{fig:flow-lens} puts
time on the x axis and transaction output sum on the y axis.

The one-year window covers about 1.06 years of recent block history. The
result has a retail sized center that gets smaller in BTC terms and a
settlement sized tail that remains large. The monthly median falls from
about 0.0019~BTC in May 2025 to about 0.00042~BTC in May 2026, and the
p90 falls from about 0.27~BTC to about 0.042~BTC. Yet the p99 remains in
multi-BTC territory, with the whole window's p99 about 18.84~BTC.
Table~\ref{tab:flow-summary} gives the one-year window values used to
read Figure~\ref{fig:flow-lens}.

output sum can be a real settlement transfer, a batch payout, a wallet
sweep, or a vault rotation. Some Ordinals and BRC-20 activity also
consumes blockspace without being a payment \cite{rodarmor2023ordinals}.
Full change adjustment and inscription filters belong to the archival
run; this one-year window figure is deliberately stated in BTC output-sum
units.

\section{Sink Lens: Age Survival Curve}\label{sec:sink}

The sink lens uses the same UTXO-age method as the stock lens, but reports
cumulative survival cutoffs instead of buckets. It asks how much of
today's BTC remains unspent after one day, one week, one month, three
months, six months, one year, two years, three years, or five years.

The cumulative pattern reinforces the stock lens. At height 950{,}696,
77.6\% of BTC is at least 180 days old, 60.5\% is at least one year old,
48.5\% is at least two years old, 43.0\% is at least three years old, and
33.3\% is at least five years old. Figure~\ref{fig:sink-lens} plots these
thresholds directly. The point is not that every old output is deliberate
cold storage; the point is that a large current stock has survived well
past daily payment horizons without being spent.

The blind spot is motive. Lost keys, omnibus custody, legal restriction,
deliberate vaulting, and ordinary long-horizon saving can look identical
in a UTXO snapshot. The evidence here is therefore an age survival
measure, not a claim about why any particular coin stayed still.

\section{Address-Level Evidence from Transaction Data}\label{sec:address-level}

This section has one purpose: it checks whether the transaction data still
look like a retail payment network after current UTXOs are grouped by
address. The answer is no. The address layer looks concentrated and
operational. A small set of recent addresses holds most address-attributable
BTC, many old addresses hold smaller balances, and a separate set of
addresses manages very large numbers of small UTXOs.

The measurement is simple. Each current UTXO has a value, creation height,
script family, and, when possible, a canonical address string. Grouping
current UTXOs by that address gives four observable facts: how much BTC an
address holds, how many current UTXOs it holds, which script family those
outputs use, and how recent the newest current UTXO at that address is. The
analysis does not identify owners or label exchanges. It only reads the
shape of the current address layer.


\subsection{Coverage and script types}

Read this subsection with Figures~\ref{fig:script-family-mix}
and~\ref{fig:script-value-per-utxo}: Figure~\ref{fig:script-family-mix}
compares UTXO-count share with BTC-balance share by script family, and
Figure~\ref{fig:script-value-per-utxo} reports BTC per current UTXO for
the same families.

The parser assigns canonical addresses to 18.31 million BTC, or 91.4\% of
the snapshot balance, across 56.21 million addresses. The remaining
1.72 million BTC is excluded from address-balance totals because those
outputs do not receive a canonical address string in this parse. The address
results therefore describe the address-attributable part of the live UTXO
set, which is most of the BTC stock.

Script family gives the protocol-level view of that stock. The categories
used here are p2pk, p2pkh, p2sh, p2wpkh, p2wsh, p2tr, multisig, and
other. Native SegWit pay-to-witness-public-key-hash holds 8.16 million BTC,
legacy pay-to-public-key-hash holds 4.64 million BTC,
pay-to-script-hash holds 3.95 million BTC, and native SegWit script-hash
holds 1.36 million BTC.
Together those mature families hold about 18.11 million BTC. P2TR is the
Pay-to-Taproot script family introduced by Taproot. It has a different
profile: 54.38 million current UTXOs, or 32.9\% of current UTXO rows, but
only 203{,}812 BTC, or 1.0\% of current BTC.
Pay-to-public-key is the opposite extreme: few current outputs but
1.72 million BTC. Figure~\ref{fig:script-family-mix} shows this full
eight-category split, and Figure~\ref{fig:script-value-per-utxo} shows the
corresponding BTC-per-UTXO scale.

The protocol categories should be read as two measurements at once. UTXO
share measures how many current output records use a script family. BTC
share measures how much value those records carry. Native SegWit p2wpkh is
large on both axes, with 29.4\% of current UTXOs and 40.8\% of current BTC.
Legacy p2pkh has 26.9\% of current UTXOs and 23.2\% of current BTC. P2SH
has a smaller output share, 7.4\%, but a larger value share, 19.7\%. P2WSH
is smaller still by output count, 1.7\%, but holds 6.8\% of current BTC.
Those mature families are the balance side of the protocol view. They hold
most of the visible BTC stock.

P2TR is the count-heavy case. It has the largest current UTXO count in
this snapshot, but the BTC held in those outputs is small. That combination
means Taproot is important for current output formation and script adoption,
not for where most spendable BTC currently sits. P2PK is the reverse
legacy case: it has very few outputs but a large balance share. The protocol
view therefore supports the same distinction used throughout the paper:
output count is activity and accounting structure; BTC balance is stored
value.

Taproot timing is reported as a P2TR-specific check, not as another script
family category. Taproot activated on 2021-11-14. The same-day protocol rows
record 208 P2TR outputs and 75 transactions spending P2TR outputs. By
2026-05-24, the daily counts are 108{,}503 P2TR outputs and 45{,}864
transactions spending P2TR outputs. Taproot therefore has visible transaction
use, but the current BTC balance remains small relative to mature script
families.

This protocol view matters because the question is not whether Bitcoin has
new script forms. It clearly does. The question is whether those script
forms change the economic reading of the base chain. The answer from the
current output set is limited but direct. P2TR is a large output-count
script family and a small balance family. P2WPKH, P2PKH, P2SH, and P2WSH are the
large balance families. P2PK is a legacy balance pocket with very little
current output formation. The script mix therefore separates innovation at
the transaction surface from the location of stored BTC.

\subsection{Address recency}

Read this subsection with Figure~\ref{fig:address-one-year} and the
recency rows of Table~\ref{tab:address-numeric}: they compare address
share with BTC share for addresses whose newest current UTXO is less than
one year old versus at least one year old.

Address recency is based on the newest current UTXO at each address. If an
address contains one five-year-old UTXO and one recent UTXO, the address is
counted as recent. This rule is strict against old-address claims, because
one new output can move the whole address into a recent category.

Under that rule, addresses with a newest current UTXO less than one year
old are 11.65 million addresses, or 20.7\% of all addresses, but they hold
15.06 million BTC, or 82.2\% of address-attributable BTC. Addresses with
no current UTXO younger than one year are 44.56 million addresses, or
79.3\% of addresses, but they hold 3.26 million BTC, or 17.8\% of
address-attributable BTC. The recency block in Table~\ref{tab:address-numeric}
shows the two-row partition; the two rows add to 100\% within that block.
Figure~\ref{fig:address-one-year} plots the same asymmetry.

The important reading is the imbalance between address count and BTC share.
A broad daily payment surface would normally make recent activity broad
across many addresses and small balances. The observed pattern is different:
a smaller recent-address set holds most address-attributable BTC, while the
larger older-address set holds much less. This does not turn recent-address
BTC into a payment measure. It shows that large balances can sit at
addresses that also receive a recent current output. That is why the recency
view is useful alongside UTXO age: one measures coin-fragment age, the
other measures address-state recency.

This is also why the address result does not reverse the stock and sink
results. An address can be classified as recent because one current output
is recent, while other outputs at the same address remain much older. The
address measure is therefore a stricter test for old-address claims and a
useful test for operational concentration. If most BTC were dispersed across
ordinary payment addresses, the recent-address share and the address-count
share would be closer together. Instead, the value share is much more
concentrated than the address share.

\subsection{UTXO frequency and balance concentration}

Read this subsection with Table~\ref{tab:address-numeric} and
Figures~\ref{fig:address-count-rank}--\ref{fig:address-rank-profiles}:
the table gives the UTXO-count buckets, and the figures separate balance
rank from output-count rank.

UTXO frequency asks how many current outputs sit at each address. A retail
payment layer would be expected to spread value broadly across many small
balances. The observed pattern is more structured. Single-UTXO addresses
are 85.3\% of addresses but hold only 26.6\% of address-attributable BTC.
Addresses with more than ten current UTXOs are only about 2.3\% of
addresses but hold 31.2\% of address-attributable BTC. Addresses with more
than one hundred UTXOs are only 0.23\% of addresses but hold 1.70 million
BTC. The UTXO-count block in Table~\ref{tab:address-numeric} is a separate
partition from the recency block; its rows also add to 100\% within that

\section{Convergence Across Lenses}\label{sec:convergence}

The stock lens and the address lens point to the same balance-side result.
Stock says that 60.5\% of current BTC is at least one year old and 43.0\%
is at least three years old. Address analysis says that the same current
stock is not spread evenly across addresses: the top 100 balance addresses
hold 3.04 million BTC, and mature script families hold most address-visible
BTC. Old coins and concentrated address balances are different measurements,
but both describe a base layer organized around stored value rather than a
large population of small working balances.

The sink lens and address recency explain why recent address activity is not
a contradiction. The sink lens shows that 33.3\% of BTC remains unspent
after at least five years. The address lens shows that 20.7\% of addresses
have a newest current UTXO under one year old and hold 82.2\% of
address-attributable BTC. Together those facts mean that large balances can
sit in addresses that occasionally receive new outputs. The result is a
ledger with old coin fragments and recent operational endpoints, not a flat
set of daily payment balances.

The flow lens and the UTXO-count address lens connect transaction size to
operational structure. Flow says the one-year transaction distribution has a
small center but a large p99 tail. Address frequency says the same chainstate
contains a many-output tail: addresses with more than ten current UTXOs are
about 2.3\% of addresses but hold 31.2\% of address-attributable BTC, while
the top 100 UTXO-count addresses contain 8.80 million current UTXOs and only
about 1{,}495 BTC. That combination is consistent with batching,
consolidation, custody operations, and settlement-sized movement.

The protocol view adds the final check. Taproot is visible in output and
spend activity, but P2TR holds only about 1.0\% of current BTC. Mature script
families hold most BTC, while newer output forms are more visible in count
than in stored value. Protocol adoption therefore changes the transaction
surface without overturning the stock, sink, flow, and address evidence.

The address lens is the bridge among the other measurements. It takes the
current UTXO stock used by the stock and sink lenses, groups it by address,
and then asks whether the live accounting surface looks broad and payment
like. It does not. The same surface has a high-balance tail, a many-output
tail, a small recent-address set that holds most value, and mature script
families that retain most BTC. Each address result lines up with one of the
macro lenses: balance concentration with stock, recency with sink, UTXO
frequency with flow, and script family with protocol adoption.

This is why the verdict does not depend on one favorite statistic. If only
UTXO age were old, the result could be dismissed as lost coins or vaulting.
If only transaction p99 were large, it could be dismissed as batching. If
only top addresses were large, it could be dismissed as exchange custody.
The combined pattern is stronger because the measurements are different
and still point to the same functional role: savings, custody, treasury
movement, output management, and settlement.

The lenses do not prove motive for any particular coin or address. Their
value is that they fail in different ways. UTXO age can overstate saving,
flow size can mix settlement with batching, and address balances can combine
many users behind one script. The verdict comes from their convergence: old
stock, survival past payment horizons, settlement-sized tails, concentrated
address balances, many-output operational addresses, and mature-script value
all point away from a base chain dominated by daily retail payments.

A daily-payment counterfactual would look different on each lens. Stock and
sink would show a smaller share of BTC surviving for years without movement.
Flow would be less dependent on a settlement-sized upper tail. Address
recency would be broad across the address population instead of concentrated
in a smaller value-heavy set. UTXO-count rank and balance rank would be less
separated. Script adoption would make new payment forms dominate both count
and value rather than mainly count. The observed data do not match that
counterfactual.

The synthesis is therefore not that Bitcoin has one use. The base chain can
still process ordinary payments, and some current outputs are undoubtedly
small payment, change, or wallet-management outputs. The claim is about the
dominant visible structure of the base layer. Across the five lenses, the
visible structure is old, concentrated, uneven, protocol-layered, and
settlement-sized at the tail. That is the empirical basis for reading the
2021--2026 base chain as store-of-value and settlement infrastructure first,
not as a daily payment rail first.

\section{Robustness, Validity, and Reproducibility}\label{sec:robustness}

The main robustness choice is to keep every claim tied to its data
boundary. Stock and sink are exact for the current chainstate snapshot,
so their uncertainty is interpretive, not sampling-based. The one-year
flow lens is exact for the one-year window after repair and verification,
but it is not the full five-year history. Address-level summaries are exact for canonical address strings assigned by the parser, but address strings are not owners. The run logs record the snapshot height, block hash, Bitcoin Core source, repaired flow rows, and sample-verification checks.

There are four threats to validity. First, age is not motive. A five-year
UTXO can be lost, legally transferred off chain, held in custody, or
intentionally saved. The paper's stock and sink claims are therefore
phrased as on-chain immobility, not as psychological intent. Second,
transaction output sums are not economic payment amounts. They include
change, batching, vault rotations, and service sweeps. This makes the
flow lens a base-layer movement-size measure, not a consumer-payment
classifier. Third, address strings are not wallets. Modern wallets rotate
addresses, while custodians and services can aggregate many users behind
one address or script policy. Fourth, the current address analysis is one-year/current-output analysis. The one-year flow window is verified, but a five-year address-flow extension requires the longer archival run.

These limitations do not erase the verdict because the main conclusion is
not a point label on any single transaction. It is a distributional
statement about the base layer. A payment-dominated base layer would be
expected to have a much smaller old-stock share, a less settlement-heavy
upper tail, and a less concentrated live address structure. The
measurements show the opposite. The safest wording is therefore functional:
the base layer currently behaves more like savings, custody, and
settlement infrastructure than like a daily payment rail.

\section{Verdict}\label{sec:verdict}

The evidence supports a scoped verdict: \btc{}'s base layer now looks more
like store-of-value and settlement infrastructure than like a daily
payment rail. The stock lens shows that 60.5\% of current BTC is at least
one year old and 43.0\% is at least three years old. The sink lens shows
that 33.3\% remains unspent after at least five years. The one-year flow
lens shows a recent transaction distribution with a small center and a
large settlement tail. The address lens shows that 18.31 million BTC maps
to 56.21 million canonical addresses, that 20.7\% of addresses had at least one current UTXO created within the past year and held 82.2\% of address-attributable BTC, and that balance and UTXO-count structure are highly concentrated.

The claim is deliberately narrower than a market narrative. It does not
say that every holder is saving, every large transaction is settlement,
or every address is a custody address. It says that the measured
Bitcoin transaction data reject the simplest daily-payment interpretation.
The base chain is still capable of payment, but its observed transaction
data are better described as an accounting layer for savings, custody,
large transfers, and operational consolidation.

The next empirical step is straightforward. The longer archival run should reuse the same schema, extend the one-year flow window backward to the full 2021--2026 range, materialize address sent-flow and change-adjusted volume, and then compare the full five-year address flow against the one-year address results here. Until that run completes, this paper is complete for current UTXO stock, current address structure, and one-year address-level analysis; it is intentionally bounded for full-history address flow.
